ARISTOTLE ON CHARACTER  A Guide for Writers & Creatives

**WHAT MAKES A CHARACTER**

**REAL**

*Aristotle**'**s Poetics — Translated for Writers, Designers **&** Storytellers*

Aristotle wrote the Poetics around 335 BCE. It is the oldest surviving work of dramatic theory in the Western tradition, and it remains one of the most useful. Aristotle was not writing theory for its own sake — he was reverse-engineering excellence. He watched the best plays of his era, asked why they worked, and wrote down his conclusions.

The original text is dense, compressed, and written for an audience that already knew Greek tragedy intimately. This guide unpacks everything Aristotle says about character — what makes one compelling, what makes one fall flat, and how the principles apply to any storytelling medium: film, television, games, or anything in between.

Nothing has been invented here. The ideas are Aristotle's. The language is ours.

# **PART ONE: THE FOUNDATION**

## **Plot is the soul. Character is the body.**

This is the most counter-intuitive thing Aristotle says, and it trips people up immediately. We live in an era that worships character psychology. Writers are told: know your character inside and out, and the story will follow. Aristotle would push back hard on this.

His argument is structural. Drama — whether a play, a film, a game — is fundamentally the imitation of an action. Not a person. An action. What happens, and what it costs.

*"**Tragedy is an imitation not of persons but of action and life, of happiness and misfortune. All human happiness or misfortune takes the form of action... Without action there cannot be a tragedy; there may be one without character.**"*

This sounds extreme. But think about what he's actually saying. A story about a fascinating person who does nothing is not a story — it's a portrait. Character only becomes meaningful when it is tested by action. We learn who someone is not from their internal monologue, but from what they do when it costs them something.

**THE CORE PRINCIPLE:  **Character is not what a person thinks or feels in private. Character is what a person does under pressure.

This is the most practically useful thing Aristotle says for writers. Before you ask "who is this character?", ask "what does this character do — and what does that action reveal?"

## **What Character Actually Does in a Story**

If plot is primary, what is character's job? Aristotle is clear: character shows moral purpose. It reveals what a person chooses when they have a genuine choice to make.

This is different from backstory, personality, quirks, or psychology. Those things can be interesting. But they are not character in Aristotle's sense. Character is revealed only in the moment of choice — specifically, a choice between two real options, where something is at stake.

A character who has no real choices is not a character. They are a prop. A character who makes choices with no consequences is not a character either. They are wish fulfillment.

**ASK YOURSELF:  **What does this character choose when both options cost them something? That answer is who they actually are.

# **PART TWO: THE FOUR REQUIREMENTS**

Aristotle gives us four explicit requirements for well-written characters. Every character you create should pass these tests.

## **1. Goodness**

This is the most misunderstood requirement. Aristotle does not mean characters must be morally virtuous. He means characters must have some moral weight — some clearly defined sense of what they value and why.

A villain can satisfy this requirement. A criminal can satisfy it. What cannot satisfy it is a character who stands for nothing — who has no values, no commitments, no stakes in anything beyond their immediate survival.

"Goodness" in Aristotle's sense means the character is oriented toward something. They care about something enough that losing it would matter. Without this, the audience has no way to engage. You cannot root for or against a person who wants nothing.

**IN PRACTICE:  **What does your character believe is worth protecting, even at personal cost? That belief is their moral core. It does not need to be admirable — it needs to exist.

## **2. Appropriateness**

Characters must fit their context convincingly. This sounds simple but has a specific meaning in Aristotle. A character's capacities, behaviors, and values must be plausible given who and where they are — their background, their role, their world.

Aristotle uses a slightly uncomfortable example: he says courage is appropriate for a man but not for a woman in his society. We can set aside the gender politics and extract the actual principle: a character's traits need to feel earned and contextually grounded. A street kid who speaks like a philosopher needs that gap to be explained. A general who crumbles at the first obstacle needs a reason.

Appropriateness is really about coherence between a character's history and their behavior. When they act, it should feel inevitable given everything we know about them — even if we're surprised.

**IN PRACTICE:  **Would this character, given everything that made them who they are, actually do this? If not, you either need a different character or a different action.

## **3. Likeness to Truth**

Aristotle calls this being "lifelike" — but he means something more precise than realistic. He means psychologically coherent. The character should behave in ways that are recognizable as human, even in extraordinary circumstances.

This is the requirement that kills most genre characters. Give a person superpowers, or magic, or extreme competence — and writers often forget to keep them psychologically real. They stop being afraid of things a person would fear. They stop wanting things a person would want. They become functions rather than people.

Likeness to truth means the character remains recognizably human in their desires, fears, contradictions, and blind spots — regardless of the world they inhabit.

**IN PRACTICE:  **Strip away the world-building and the powers. Does this character still have recognizable human drives — love, fear, ambition, shame, grief? If not, they fail this requirement.

## **4. Consistency**

Aristotle's fourth requirement is the most nuanced, and the most useful. A character must be consistent — or if inconsistent, consistently inconsistent.

What he means is this: a character's behavior must follow a logic. We must be able to predict how they would behave in new situations, because we understand the underlying pattern. If a character acts one way in act one and a completely different way in act three, we need to understand why. If we do not, we feel cheated.

But — and this is where it gets interesting — a character who is consistently unpredictable is still consistent. A character who always acts against their own interests, or always makes impulsive decisions, has a consistent pattern. The audience learns to anticipate the unpredictability. That is its own form of coherence.

What breaks consistency is unmotivated change. A character suddenly becoming generous, cowardly, or cruel without the story earning it. That is not character development — it is character replacement.

**IN PRACTICE:  **Can you describe your character's underlying behavioral logic in one sentence? If you can, you can test any action against it. If you cannot, neither can your audience.

# **PART THREE: HAMARTIA**

## **The Concept That Changes Everything**

Hamartia is the most famous and most misused idea in the Poetics. It is usually translated as "tragic flaw" — and that translation has done enormous damage to how writers think about character.

The word does not mean flaw. It comes from archery. It means "missing the mark" — a specific error, at a specific moment, that sends everything in the wrong direction.

This is not a personality defect. It is not something wrong with a person's character in a general sense. It is a mistake — usually a comprehensible, even understandable one — made at a critical juncture.

*"**The change of fortune... must not be the fall of a great man into misery from wickedness and vice, but rather the contrary, the fall of a good man from happiness into misery by some error.**"*

## **Why the ****"****Tragic Flaw****"**** Translation is Damaging**

When writers think about tragic flaws, they tend to give characters a personality defect: arrogance, jealousy, recklessness. Then they construct a story where that defect predictably destroys the character. The audience can see it coming from the first act.

This produces morality tales, not tragedies. The character becomes a cautionary example rather than a person. We watch them fail without real grief because we knew it was coming — it was written into their nature from the beginning.

Aristotle's hamartia is different in a crucial way: it is not a flaw the character always had. It is a specific error of judgment made at a specific moment, often under pressure, often from a position of genuine virtue.

**THE DISTINCTION:  **"Tragic flaw" thinking: this person is arrogant, so they fall. Hamartia thinking: this person, who is genuinely great, makes one specific comprehensible mistake — and it destroys everything.

## **The Flaw and the Gift are the Same Thing**

Here is where it becomes genuinely powerful for writers. The best hamartia is not separate from what makes a character great — it emerges from it. The strength and the wound are the same thing operating in different circumstances.

Oedipus is relentlessly curious, intellectually fearless, refuses to let a mystery remain unsolved. This is what makes him a great king. It is also what destroys him — he pursues the truth until the truth kills him. His greatness and his undoing are a single characteristic.

Macbeth's fierce ambition and decisive action — the qualities that make him an exceptional warrior — are exactly what make him a catastrophic king.

In more contemporary terms: the detective who cannot let a case go past the point of sanity. The soldier whose absolute loyalty to their unit makes them willing to cross lines that should never be crossed. The parent whose love becomes control.

**THE QUESTION:  **What is the best thing about your character? Now ask: under what specific circumstances does that exact quality become the thing that destroys them?

## **Hamartia and Moral Ambiguity**

One of the most important functions of hamartia is that it prevents both pure villainy and pure heroism. Aristotle is explicit that a completely good person falling into misery is not tragic — it is merely distressing. And a bad person getting their just deserts is not tragic either — it is satisfying, but not moving.

The tragic character must be someone we recognize as fundamentally decent, facing a situation where their specific configuration of virtues and limitations leads them to an error. We cannot fully condemn them because we understand — maybe even share — the quality that led them wrong.

This is why hamartia produces empathy in a way that "tragic flaw" does not. We do not pity someone for being arrogant. We pity someone for being too loyal, too honest, too thorough — for the error that their best quality made inevitable.

# **PART FOUR: RECOGNITION AND REVERSAL**

## **The Two Mechanisms of Character**

Aristotle identifies two specific plot mechanics that are most powerful for revealing character. They are related — and at their best, they happen simultaneously.

## **Peripeteia — Reversal of Fortune**

Peripeteia is the moment when the action turns. What was going toward success suddenly moves toward disaster — or vice versa. Importantly, Aristotle specifies that the reversal should arise from the action itself, not from external chance.

In other words, the character's own actions should produce the reversal. The moves they made to secure their position should be the moves that undermine it. This is not just good plotting — it is the mechanism by which character and consequence become inseparable.

A reversal that comes from outside — a random accident, a coincidence, an unforeseeable external event — may produce change, but it does not reveal character. It just happens to the character. A reversal that comes from the character's own choices reveals everything about who they are.

**IN PRACTICE:  **The best reversals are ones where the audience can look back and see exactly how the character's own logic led here — even if they didn't see it coming.

## **Anagnorisis — Recognition**

Anagnorisis is the moment of recognition — when a character moves from ignorance to knowledge. In Greek tragedy this was often literal: Oedipus recognizing that the man he killed was his father, that the woman he married was his mother.

But the principle extends far beyond literal recognition. It is the moment when a character understands something true about their situation — or about themselves — that they did not understand before. And crucially, Aristotle says this recognition should change everything.

The most powerful dramatic moments in any medium tend to be anagnorisis moments. The realization. The truth that cannot be unfaced. These moments work because they combine knowledge with consequence — understanding something changes what must happen next.

When peripeteia and anagnorisis coincide — when the reversal of fortune and the moment of recognition are the same event — Aristotle says this is the highest form of dramatic construction. The character's fall and their understanding of why it happened are one and the same moment.

**IN PRACTICE:  **What does your character need to understand about themselves for the story to be complete? When they finally understand it — does that understanding change anything, or come too late to matter?

# **PART FIVE: CHARACTER REVEALED THROUGH ACTION**

## **Show, Don****'****t Explain — Two Thousand Years Ago**

Aristotle makes a point that modern writing teachers repeat constantly, without knowing its source: character is not declared, it is demonstrated.

He is skeptical of character description — of being told what a person is like. What matters is what they do. A character described as courageous must do something courageous at a moment when courage costs them something. Otherwise the description is decorative.

This is especially important for games and interactive media, where the player is making choices on behalf of a character. The actions available in any given moment are the character. The architecture of choice is character design.

In film and television, this principle means: resist explaining characters through dialogue. If a character tells us who they are, we are being told a story about them. If we watch them choose, we are experiencing their character directly.

**THE TEST:  **Remove every line of dialogue where a character explains themselves or their motivations. Does the character still make sense? Do they still have depth? If yes, the explaining was unnecessary. If no — you have work to do on the actions.

## **The Three Types of Characters to Avoid**

Aristotle identifies three types of character that fail dramatically, and his reasons are as useful now as they were in 335 BCE.

**The Perfectly Good Person**

A character who is entirely virtuous, who makes no real errors, who suffers through no fault of their own — produces distress in the audience, not tragedy. We feel bad for them, but we cannot learn from them. There is no recognition available to us. Their suffering is simply unfair, and unfairness produces anger, not catharsis.

More practically: perfect characters are boring because they have no internal conflict. Conflict must come entirely from external sources because they cannot generate it themselves. They become passive — things happen to them rather than through them.

**The Purely Wicked Person**

A villain who is evil without comprehensible motivation, who wants destruction for its own sake, who has no dimension of recognizable humanity — satisfies us when they fail but does not move us. We feel no complexity about their defeat.

This produces entertainment without meaning. We root against them as we root against a chess opponent — strategically, not emotionally. Their destruction does not teach us anything about the human condition because they are not recognizably human.

**The Merely Mediocre**

Perhaps Aristotle's most underappreciated category. A character who is simply ordinary — no particular strength, no particular flaw, no particular stakes — is not tragic, not comic, not anything. They are background. The audience cannot attach to them because there is nothing to attach to.

Mediocre characters are the default failure mode of inexperienced writers. They are created through avoidance — avoiding anything too extreme, too specific, too committed. The result is a character who is technically present but narratively absent.

**THE SOLUTION:  **Commit. A character with a specific, even uncomfortable virtue or flaw is always more interesting than a character who is averagely decent. Specificity is not the same as extremity — but it is the same as aliveness.

# **PART SIX: THE PRACTICAL CHECKLIST**

## **Building a Character Through Aristotle****'****s Lens**

These questions are not a formula. They are pressure tests. Apply them to any character you are building and see where the structure holds and where it gives way.

**On Foundation**

- What does this character do — specifically — when the story's central pressure falls on them?

- What does their action reveal about what they value?

- Is their moral weight clear, even if their morality is ambiguous?

**On the Four Requirements**

- Goodness: What does this character stand for? What would they protect even at cost?

- Appropriateness: Does their behavior cohere with their history and context?

- Likeness to Truth: Are they recognizably human in their fears, wants, and contradictions?

- Consistency: Can you state their behavioral logic in one sentence?

**On Hamartia**

- What is the best thing about this character?

- How does that exact quality become the mechanism of their greatest mistake?

- Is their error comprehensible — could we imagine making it ourselves?

- Does their fall produce recognition, or just misfortune?

**On Action and Revelation**

- Can you cut every line where they explain themselves — and still understand them?

- What is the one moment of recognition available to this character?

- Do they reach it — and does it come too late, or just in time, or not at all?

- Do their own actions produce the reversal, or does it come from outside?

**On What to Avoid**

- Are they too perfect? Give them a genuine error, not a cosmetic flaw.

- Are they too purely evil? Find the comprehensible logic underneath.

- Are they too mediocre? Find one specific thing that makes them irreplaceable in this story.

# **CLOSING NOTE**

Aristotle was not trying to make rules. He was trying to describe what excellence looked like — and why it worked. The Poetics is not a formula for great storytelling. It is a map of the territory.

The principles here are not period-specific. They apply to Greek tragedy because Aristotle observed them there. They apply to The Godfather, to Breaking Bad, to The Last of Us, to any story that has ever made an audience feel something real — because they describe something true about how human beings process narrative and meaning.

A character who acts. Who chooses at cost. Who is specific, coherent, and human. Whose best quality contains their worst error. Who either recognizes what they have done or fails to — and either way, something true about existence is revealed.

That is still the standard. It was the standard 2,400 years ago. It will likely still be the standard in another 2,400.

*"**The poet**'**s function is to describe, not the thing that has happened, but a kind of thing that might happen — what is possible as being probable or necessary.**"**  — Aristotle, Poetics*
