ARISTOTLE ON DIALOGUE

*Voice, Thought **&** the Architecture of Dramatic Speech — A Guide for Writers **&** Creatives*

Dialogue is fourth on Aristotle's list. Behind plot, character, and thought. This is not a dismissal — it is a structural instruction. Dialogue serves the three elements above it. It cannot substitute for any of them. Understanding what dialogue can and cannot do, in Aristotle's framework, is the difference between writing scenes that crackle and writing scenes that explain.

This guide draws on the Poetics and on the Rhetoric — Aristotle's manual for persuasion, which turns out to be the most practical source we have on how characters speak under pressure.

**PART ONE: WHERE DIALOGUE SITS**

## Diction, Thought, and Character Are Not the Same Thing

Aristotle's six elements of drama — plot, character, thought, diction, song, spectacle — form a hierarchy. Diction (lexis in Greek) is verbal expression: the actual words spoken. It is fourth because it serves the three elements above it.

The most important distinction for writers: Aristotle separates character (ethos) from thought (dianoia). They are different things, revealed through different means.

Ethos — character — is revealed by moral choices. By what a person does when something is at stake. You cannot talk your way into demonstrating ethos. You have to choose.

Dianoia — thought — is revealed by speech. By what a person argues, declares, believes, reasons about. Dianoia is the intellectual content of what characters say. It tells us what they think. It does not tell us who they are.

The practical implication: a character who speaks at length about their own values, motivations, and inner life is producing dianoia. Not ethos. What they say about themselves may or may not be true. It is always, in some sense, a performance — to themselves, to the other characters, to the audience. The audience should be watching for the gap between what is said and what is done. That gap is where the actual character lives.

**THE CORE PRINCIPLE:  **Dialogue tells us what characters think. Action tells us who they are. A character's account of themselves is data about their self-image — not a reliable report on their character.

**PART TWO: THE THREE MODES OF SPEECH**

## Ethos, Pathos, Logos in Every Conversation

Aristotle's Rhetoric identifies three modes of persuasion. They are designed to analyze public speech and argument, but they map directly onto how characters speak to each other under dramatic pressure.

The first mode is ethos — the speaker's credibility, authority, and character. When a character speaks from ethos, they are saying: trust me because of who I am. Their position, their track record, their identity gives weight to their words. The general who gives the order. The parent who invokes their love. The expert who cites their training.

The second mode is pathos — emotional appeal to the listener. When a character speaks from pathos, they are saying: feel what I feel. They are attempting to create an emotional state in the other person that makes compliance or agreement feel natural. Pathos is the mode of the desperate, the vulnerable, and the manipulative — often simultaneously.

The third mode is logos — logical argument. Evidence, reasoning, cause and effect. When a character speaks from logos, they are saying: here is the thinking, here are the facts. Logos is the mode of characters who believe that reason will win the day — and the mode of characters who use the appearance of reason to conceal something else.

Every significant line of dialogue is operating in one or more of these modes, whether the writer intends it or not. Making the choice consciously changes the writing.

*"**Of the modes of persuasion furnished by the spoken word there are three kinds. The first kind depends on the personal character of the speaker; the second on putting the audience into a certain frame of mind; the third on the proof provided by the words of the speech itself. — Aristotle, Rhetoric**"*

The character-revealing question is not which mode a character uses — it is which mode they default to under pressure. A character who always argues from authority reveals their relationship to power. A character who always argues from emotion reveals how much they have learned to weaponize vulnerability. A character who always argues from logic reveals either genuine analytical habit or a deep fear of emotional exposure.

And the most revealing moment: when a character is losing an argument, what mode do they retreat to? The person who starts with logos and ends with ethos ('you should trust me') is telling you something essential about their underlying insecurity. The person who starts with pathos and ends with logos ('let me explain why this is actually rational') is telling you something essential about their relationship to their own feelings.

**THE MODE TEST:  **What mode does this character default to under pressure? What mode do they retreat to when they're losing? The gap between entry mode and exit mode is character.

**PART THREE: WHAT DIALOGUE CANNOT DO**

## The Four Things Writers Ask Dialogue to Do That It Cannot

Dialogue cannot establish character. Action establishes character. Dialogue can claim character — and the claim can be wrong. A character who says 'I'm not the kind of person who gives up' and then gives up has told the audience something true, but not what they intended. The action always overrides the declaration.

Dialogue cannot create stakes. Stakes are established by what choices cost — by what the character has to lose, and what they actually lose. Dialogue can name the cost. Characters can tell each other and the audience what is at risk. But if the cost isn't built into the structure, naming it doesn't make it real. The audience will not feel what the characters are claiming they should feel.

Dialogue cannot substitute for plot. A scene in which two characters discuss what has happened, or what might happen, without making any choice that changes the situation — is not a dramatic scene. It is an explanation. Explanatory scenes are sometimes necessary. They are never sufficient.

Dialogue cannot save a character who hasn't been built. A writer who reaches the third act and realizes the audience doesn't understand a character will often try to fix it through a confessional speech. This almost never works. Character is built through accumulated action. A speech that explains who someone is, without a preceding pattern of choices to anchor it, reads as invention — not revelation.

**THE SCENE TEST:  **Remove this scene. Does the story's action change? If not, what is the scene actually doing? Name that function precisely. If you cannot name it, consider whether the scene is necessary.

**PART FOUR: SUBTEXT AND THE GAP**

## What Characters Don't Say

Aristotle doesn't use the word subtext — it's a twentieth-century term — but his framework demands it. If ethos is revealed by action, not speech; and if dianoia can be wrong about itself; then the most revealing dramatic dialogue is dialogue that shows the audience what the character is not saying.

Subtext emerges from the gap. Between what a character says and what they choose. Between what they claim to want and what they actually pursue. Between the mode of speech they present and the need underneath it. The audience, watching and listening simultaneously, perceives both signal and noise. When the two are dissonant, the dissonance is the truth.

A character who says 'I'm fine' after something terrible is not lying, exactly. They are protecting something. The audience sees both the claim and the wound, and the gap between them is more information than either alone. A character who makes a logical argument for a decision that is visibly emotionally driven is not deceiving the other characters, necessarily. They may be deceiving themselves. The audience sees both, and their knowledge of what the character doesn't know is the source of dramatic irony.

The practical implication for writing dialogue: resist the urge to have characters say the true thing directly. The true thing said directly is dianoia — it tells the audience what the character thinks. The true thing visible beneath what the character actually says is ethos — it shows the audience who the character is.

**THE SUBTEXT PRINCIPLE:  **A character who says exactly what they mean is less interesting than a character who says something adjacent to what they mean, and whose real meaning is visible to the audience but not to the other characters in the scene.

**PART FIVE: DIALOGUE UNDER PRESSURE**

## When Speech Becomes Action

Aristotle's core principle — character is revealed under pressure, not in comfort — applies directly to how dialogue functions dramatically. A conversation where nothing is at stake is not a dramatic scene. Dialogue becomes dramatic when it is a form of action: when what gets said changes something, costs something, commits someone to a position they cannot easily retreat from.

The pressure in a dialogue scene can take many forms. A character can be forced to choose, in speech, between two loyalties. A secret can be on the verge of exposure. A relationship can be renegotiating its terms in real time. One character can have power over another and be deciding whether to use it. What all these situations share: the words spoken carry consequence. The characters are not exchanging information. They are making moves.

Pressure in dialogue also reveals which characters listen and which perform. A character under pressure who is genuinely listening — who is changed, even slightly, by what the other person says — is doing something different from a character who is simply waiting for the other to finish so they can say their prepared thing. Aristotle's framework implies that genuine dramatic exchange requires both characters to be at risk. If only one character has something to lose in the conversation, it is not a scene — it is a demonstration.

**THE PRESSURE TEST:  **What does each character need from this conversation? What would it cost them to say the true thing? What do they say instead — and why? Does the conversation change the situation, or describe it?

**PART SIX: MONOLOGUE**

## Thought in Direct Address

Monologue is the character speaking directly — to themselves, to another character, to the audience. In Aristotelian terms, monologue is the purest form of dianoia. Thought expressed without the filter of another character's response.

The danger is obvious: a monologue that explains the character removes the gap. It gives the audience what they are supposed to discover. The audience becomes passive — they are told who someone is, rather than seeing it for themselves. This produces the least engaged response available to a storyteller.

What makes monologue powerful is not self-revelation but self-misunderstanding. The character thinks they are explaining themselves. The audience sees what they are actually revealing. Hamlet's soliloquies are not confessional accurately — they are a man in motion, arguing with himself, and the argument reveals more than any straightforward self-description could. He is wrong about himself in the exact ways that matter.

Monologue also has a specific structural function that dialogue cannot perform: it shows a character's interior logic operating without the pressure of another person. We see how they think when unobserved. This is distinct from what they do when observed — and the gap between those two things is, again, character.

The question to ask of any monologue: what does this character believe they are doing in this speech? Justifying themselves? Preparing for something? Grieving? Working through a problem? Now ask: what are they actually doing? What does this speech reveal about them that they do not intend to reveal? If the two answers are the same, the monologue is explaining. If they are different, the monologue is working.

**THE MONOLOGUE TEST:  **What does the character believe they are saying? What is the audience actually learning? The gap between those two answers is the monologue's dramatic content.

**PART SEVEN: VOICE AND THE FOUR REQUIREMENTS**

## How Aristotle's Requirements Shape How a Character Speaks

Goodness — moral core — shapes what a character is willing to say and what they will never say, regardless of pressure. A character with a defined moral core has verbal limits. There are arguments they won't make. There are positions they won't take even when it would help them. These refusals, when they come at cost, are the most revealing things a character can do in dialogue.

Appropriateness shapes vocabulary, register, and rhetorical habit. A character speaks in ways that are plausible given their history and world. This is not just dialect or education — it is the categories they think in, the comparisons they naturally reach for, the arguments they find compelling. A person raised in a particular world will argue from the premises of that world, even when arguing against it.

Likeness to truth keeps voice recognizably human. In extraordinary circumstances, characters still speak from fear, desire, shame, and love. Strip away the world and the plot: does this character still sound like a person navigating real emotional stakes? If they sound like a plot mechanism delivering necessary information, they fail this requirement.

Consistency — behavioral logic — means a character's voice should be recognizable across scenes. Not identical: a character can speak differently under different pressures, in different relationships, with different stakes. But the underlying pattern — the rhythm of thought, the default mode, the characteristic evasion — should be consistent enough that the audience could predict it.

**VOICE PRINCIPLE:  **A character's voice is not style. It is the sound of their specific configuration of values, fears, and habits under the particular pressure of this scene. Get the character right, and the voice follows.

**PART EIGHT: THE PRACTICAL CHECKLIST**

## Dialogue Tests for Every Scene

**ON THE SCENE**

— What does each character need from this conversation?

— What would it cost each character to say the true thing?

— What do they say instead — and what does that choice reveal?

— Does the scene end with the situation changed? If not, what does it accomplish?

**ON INDIVIDUAL LINES**

— Is this line dianoia (what the character thinks) or ethos (who they are through action)?

— What mode is the character using — ethos, pathos, or logos?

— Is the character saying what they mean, or something adjacent? What is the gap?

— Could you cut this line without losing anything? If yes, consider cutting it.

**ON MONOLOGUE**

— What does the character believe they are doing in this speech?

— What is the audience actually learning that the character does not intend to reveal?

— Is the monologue explanation (the gap is absent) or revelation (the gap is the content)?

**ON VOICE**

— Does this character's vocabulary and rhetorical habit fit their history and world?

— Under pressure, which mode do they default to? Which do they retreat to when losing?

— Is their voice consistent enough that the audience could predict how they would speak in a new scene?

— Strip away the world and the plot. Does this character still sound like a person with real emotional stakes?

**ON DIALOGUE AND CHARACTER**

— Remove every line where a character explains themselves or their motivations. Does the character still make sense? If yes, the explanation was unnecessary.

— Find the moment in the scene where the situation changes. Is it caused by a speech act — a commitment, a refusal, a revelation? If not, find the moment of change and build toward it.

— What is the truest thing this character could say in this scene? Do they say it? If not — why not, and what do they say instead? Both the answer and the evasion are character.
