ARISTOTLE ON ROLE

*Lead, Supporting **&** Minor Characters — A Guide for Writers **&** Creatives*

Aristotle never wrote a theory of supporting characters. His unit of analysis was always the action — the spine of what happens and what it costs. But the Poetics implies a clear structural hierarchy for everyone who appears in a story. It is not about screen time. It is not about likability. It is about causal weight: how much of the story's central action runs through a given character.

This guide translates that hierarchy into practical terms for writers working in any medium.

**PART ONE: THE STRUCTURAL FOUNDATION**

## Role is Determined by Causality

The lead character is whoever generates the story's central reversal through their own choices. If you can remove a character and the spine of the story remains intact — the sequence of events, the central error, the consequences — they are not the lead. They may be important. They may be beloved. But the action does not run through them.

This is Aristotle's hardest test for character hierarchy, and it cuts through most instinctive thinking about 'the main character.' The protagonist is not whoever appears most, or whoever the audience likes best. The protagonist is whoever makes the choice that sets everything irreversibly in motion.

**THE TEST:  **Remove this character. Does the central action still happen, and still mean the same thing? If yes, they are not the lead.

**PART TWO: THE LEAD**

## Full Aristotelian Treatment

The lead character requires all four of Aristotle's requirements — goodness, appropriateness, likeness to truth, consistency — and a complete hamartia construction. They must be capable of peripeteia (the reversal of fortune that emerges from their own choices) and anagnorisis (the moment of recognition, when they understand what they have done or failed to do).

What distinguishes the lead from everyone else is not virtue or complexity. It is that their specific configuration of strengths and limitations must be the engine that drives the story to its outcome. Their hamartia is not decorative — it is structural. The story arrives where it does because this person, with this particular quality pressed to this particular limit, made this particular error.

The lead must also be capable of suffering the full consequences of that error. Not in a punitive sense — Aristotle is clear that suffering is not the point. But the reversal must land on them fully. A character insulated from the consequences of their own choices cannot be a lead in Aristotle's sense. They are a spectator to their own story.

*"**The change of fortune must not be the fall of a great man into misery from wickedness, but rather the fall of a good man from happiness into misery by some error. — Aristotle, Poetics**"*

**BUILDING THE LEAD:  **What choice, made from genuine strength, produces the reversal? That choice and the quality that drives it are the heart of your lead character.

**PART THREE: THE DEUTERAGONIST**

## The Second Substantial Character

Aristotle doesn't use this term, but the Poetics requires this character. When two substantial figures share the dramatic weight of a story — Antigone and Creon, Walter and Jesse, Hamlet and Claudius — each needs full hamartia construction. The deuteragonist is not a supporting character. They are a second engine in the same machine.

The crucial structural requirement: the deuteragonist's hamartia should be complementary to the lead's. Not identical — complementary. The two characters' specific qualities should intersect in a way that makes the central conflict not just possible but inevitable. If you could swap one of them out for a different character without changing the story's logic, the relationship isn't doing its full structural work.

In practice: the deuteragonist often provides the pressure that activates the lead's hamartia. Or they represent the road not taken — a person with a different configuration of the same qualities, whose different error illuminates the lead's by contrast. Either way, their arc is not subordinate to the lead's. It is intertwined with it.

**THE TEST:  **Does the deuteragonist's specific error make the lead's error more inevitable? If yes, the structural relationship is working.

**PART FOUR: SUPPORTING CHARACTERS**

## The Four Requirements Without the Full Arc

Supporting characters need goodness, appropriateness, likeness to truth, and consistency. What they do not need is a full hamartia arc. Their function is not to generate the central reversal — it is to reveal the lead.

Aristotle is clear that character is revealed in relation: to choices, to pressure, to other people. A supporting character's job is to create the conditions under which the lead's character becomes visible. They do this by presenting genuine choices, by applying pressure, by representing values or stakes that the lead must respond to. They are, in the best sense, instruments of revelation.

This does not mean they are simple or thin. A supporting character can be fully realized — complex, specific, psychologically coherent — without needing their own complete arc. The difference is that their arc serves the central action rather than being the central action.

The practical test: a supporting character who could be removed without affecting the lead's choices and development is not a supporting character. They are a background character. Find the moment where they change something for the lead — the choice they force, the truth they reveal, the relationship that costs the lead something. That is their reason for existing.

**BUILDING SUPPORT:  **What does this character reveal about the lead that could not be revealed any other way? That function is their structural role.

**PART FIVE: MINOR CHARACTERS**

## Functional Presence

Minor characters exist to make specific moments possible. They do not need a hamartia arc. They do not need a full psychological interior. What they need is appropriateness and consistency: their behavior must make sense given who they are, and must remain predictable within the logic of their world.

The failure mode with minor characters is not thinness — it is inconsistency. A character who appears in one scene and behaves in a way that contradicts the world, or that feels unmotivated by anything recognizably human, breaks plausibility even if they disappear immediately. The audience's trust in the story's reality is constructed cumulatively, and minor characters are part of that construction.

A well-written minor character feels like a person with an entire life offscreen, even if we see only one moment of it. This is not achieved through backstory or explanation — it is achieved through behavioral specificity. They want something in this scene. It may be small. But it is real, and it shapes how they act.

**THE TEST:  **Can you predict how this minor character would behave in any situation relevant to their scene? If yes, they are consistent. Consistency is all they need.

**PART SIX: ENSEMBLE CASTS**

## When the Lead is Distributed

Some stories distribute the weight of the central action across multiple characters. No single person generates the reversal — the ensemble does, collectively. In Aristotelian terms, each substantial member of the ensemble carries a fraction of the protagonist function.

The structural requirement for ensemble storytelling: each major ensemble member needs their own compressed version of the Aristotelian arc. Not a full tragedy or comedy — but a recognizable mini-structure where their specific quality creates a specific error, and they either do or do not recognize it. The ensemble's collective hamartia is often a shared assumption or worldview that the story dismantles.

The danger in ensemble writing is diffusion — spread the action too evenly and no single reversal carries enough weight to produce catharsis. The solution Aristotle implies: even in an ensemble, there should be a moment when the full weight of consequence lands somewhere. Identify where the story finally breaks open, and who is standing at the center of it. That character is doing lead-level work in that moment, even if they've been ensemble for the rest of the story.

**ENSEMBLE PRINCIPLE:  **Find the moment the story finally breaks open. Whoever stands at the center of that moment is carrying the protagonist function, regardless of how evenly the story has been distributed before.

**PART SEVEN: THE PRACTICAL CHECKLIST**

## Role Tests for Every Character

**ON LEAD CHARACTERS**

— Does the central reversal emerge from this character's own choices and errors?

— Is their hamartia structural — does it make the story's outcome inevitable?

— Are they capable of anagnorisis — of understanding, even partially, what they have done?

— Do the full consequences of their error land on them?

**ON DEUTERAGONISTS**

— Is their hamartia complementary to the lead's — not identical, but interlocked?

— Does their specific error make the lead's error more inevitable?

— Could you swap them out for a different character without changing the story's logic? If yes, the relationship isn't doing its full work.

**ON SUPPORTING CHARACTERS**

— What do they reveal about the lead that cannot be revealed any other way?

— Do they force at least one genuine choice for the lead?

— Could they be removed without affecting the lead's development? If yes, find their structural function or cut them.

**ON MINOR CHARACTERS**

— Do they want something in their scene, even if it's small?

— Is their behavior consistent with their world and apparent history?

— Does their presence make the specific moment they appear in more real?

**ON ENSEMBLES**

— Does each major ensemble member have a compressed arc — quality, error, recognition?

— Is there a collective hamartia the group shares?

— Find the moment the story breaks open. Who is there? Make sure that character carries the full weight.
